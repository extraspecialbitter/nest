Dear Friends and Fellow Sculptors of the Microcosmic World known as The East - West '99 Renku,

I have for you the ageku, the final piece of the puzzle, we have so enthusiastically assembled. Of the twenty-six candidates submitted, many of which were very, very good, I felt that there were three outstanding stanzas. After careful scrutiny the chosen one leaped out to take its place as the final poem of East - West '99. The honor goes to Sandra Fuhringer for her verse:

a door standing open
to the slow spring rain

It is an invitation to enjoy eternity, to appreciate our wealth of opportunities, an invitation, not only to the newlyweds in Kiyoko's stanza, but to us all. This verse has such powerful resonance! It carries us, from the life time of East - West '99 right out through the open door, into the spring rain. Sandra welcomes the rain, to delight our senses, to see, hear, and smell it, to become wet by it. What a welcoming of life. Many thanks go to Sandra for this open-hearted embrace.

We have now finished what we started out to do, create a kasen renku of historic proportions. East - West '99 began with the first ever hokku contest outside of Japan. It then continued at Haiku North America in Evanston, Illinois, garnering several verses and recruiting a few curious poets. After the conference, verses were added via e-mail until Saturday, July 31, when we held the second ever live-on-line renku party. Present at the party were two groups of poets (one in Richmond, California, and one in Port Townsend, Washington), and several individuals, all told about seventeen poets. The party lasted for more than nine hours, during which nineteen verses were added. In addition to the two groups, there were poets on-line in Maine, Florida, Washington, California, and Canada. And that was only during the live session!

In reading our poem, I get the clear sense that we have been very successful indeed. My feelings at this point are, first of all, deep gratitude, and second, emptiness, knowing that are journey together, at least for now, has reached a conclusion, that I won't be coming to my computer (several times a day) and finding ingenious, or beautiful, or hilarious stanzas from you.

For me this has been an amazing month and a half. I want to thank each and every one who participated, whether you have a stanza visibly in this poem or not. This morning, in reading East - West '99 again very slowly, I consciously paused at each verse, at each new name, and reflected on who had written it, the person, and the verse or verses he or she sent to me, the notes, the questions, the comments having to do with those stanzas. And then I bowed. It was a very wonderful experience. I tell you this because I want you all to know that I honor, and respect, and thank you individually as well as collectively.

All renku are like the tips of icebergs; we only see the verses accepted or agreed upon. For every verse you read there are others that had to be passed over for some reason or other. Many of the ones you don't see are superb verses. In East - West '99, on the average, there were about ten verses rejected for each one accepted (not including the two verses that were exclusive: my wakiku, and Kiyoko Tokutomi's cherry blossom stanza, #35). There were approximately 362 submissions in all. Forty-three poets joined in the effort; twenty-seven have poems in the final copy. Nineteen poets have one stanza included, seven poets have two, and one ingenious soul somehow managed to muster three stanzas. The most important thing I hope you realise about this is that, included or not, every single verse helped make this poem what it is; all are very real parts of the whole renku which, in itself, has much more to do with process than product. Whether you submitted just one verse without acceptance, or fifteen, or forty verses with a single acceptance, you contributed time, energy, thought, and caring to a project that involved people working together to achieve collaborative harmony.

Part of my feeling of gratitude is due to some wonderful new insights that came to me as I struggled and laughed my way through the construction of East - West '99. These insights have to do with the meaning and purpose of renku, and with what it means to organize and manage them. I doubt that I can put these visions into words adequately right now, but I hope to. Briefly however, I'll say this: renku is difficult, sometimes frustrating, and sometimes rewarding, but mostly it's fun, and what a marvelous education! Each stanza in a renku has an important purpose, and someone is appointed or selected to fullfill that purpose. Every renku is a life. Born in a hokku, it develops through a series of emotional experiences, reaches maturity and, in passing away, is joyfully released into the realm of infinite possibility.

–Christopher Herold
Sunday, August 15, 1999
