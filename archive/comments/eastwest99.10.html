Hello everyone,

    Okay. Another difficult decision for the guy in the hot seat. And four more top verses, each of which could take the renku into a new direction and feel. And no, I'm not going to split this beast into four streams.

    Doris Thurston had yet another good link, this one of the narrative sort:

first light–
their tangled pajamas

    This verse would wrap up the love sequence well. The ambiguity in Kay's verse (our not knowing whether the man unlocking the door is welcome or not) would be resolved. Doris points to a happy outcome, one in which the couple spends the night making love and awakens in the resultant disorder. Unfortunately, there is already an article of clothing in the renku, in verse three ( bedroom slippers). Doris initially wrote of "yukata," lightweight cotton kimonos worn during the summer season. Summer wouldn't work well at this stage in our poem, so I asked her to make the change to pajamas, and she agreed. Then I noticed the clothing repetition. If not for this glitch, Doris' stanza would have been a fine addition.

    Francine Porad came up with an adroit shift from the circumstance at hand. She took the position that no visitors were welcome at the time the man arrives in Kay's verse. Francine's

phone off the hook
TV cop with answers

indicates (I know 'cause she told me) that when watching the news, or about to hear answers to some puzzling point of interest, she does NOT want to be disturbed, so, she takes the phone off the hook. But that doesn't work when someone is coming into the room. Therefore the man's presence becomes an annoyance.

    Jane Reichold submitted an excellent verse also, one that brings clarity to the moment and adds intensity to an already intense scene.

cuckoo! cuckoo! cuckoo!
from the hallway clock

    The woman in Kay's verse is now be doubly startled, by the key in the lock and, simultaneously, by the jarring sound of the cuckoo clock. From one view point there seems to be something rather ominous about this moment, as if it foreshadows an unpleasant situation. The cuckoo has a certain craziness about it. Could the man be abusive? Could the woman be insanely jealous? However this combination of verses hits you, whether implying danger, comic relief, or simply startlement, the moment becomes much more vivid. This verse could work very well, and my only reason for choosing someone else's is subjective, an inclination to move into an alternative emotional territory.

    Alice Benedict's

left on the dining table
lucky end of the wishbone

is the verse, and the mood, I decided would become the tenth stanza. This two-liner does so much! First of all, someone's wish comes true, and that wish has something to do with the man's entrance (through Kay's verse) . . . turning her toward him. The wish (for intimacy it seems) becomes reality, and, in the excitement of the moment, the wishbone is left on the table. By the time we (the readers) actually see the wishbone, it is probably already the next morning and, through Alice's well-crafted, subtle implication, we have a pretty good idea of what went on the night before. This stanza (as would Doris Thurston's "tangled pajamas") gives a positive "turn" to the unfolding drama. We are now left wide open to shift away from love, to a verse with no seasonal reference. I'm also seeking to move away from human affairs in general for a bit, since people have been dominant in the last four stanzas.
