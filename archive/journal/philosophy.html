Introduction

The Heron's Nest is a journal of haiku and linked verse that embody traditional spirit. As editor of this journal, I'd like you to know my thoughts about haiku so that you can decide if you'd like to visit more often, or perhaps submit your work to me. I also want to tell you about The Heron's Nest live-on-line chat room in which we can regularly discuss, and write haikai forms together. First of all, here are some of my thoughts about haiku. They'll help you get to know what I look for in the work that comes to me.

As you probably know, there are long lists of dos and don'ts associated with haiku, set forth by the Japanese who originated the form, and by westerners attempting to emulate them. And there is, of course, much difference of opinion as to the relative importance of these dos and don'ts. Which, if any, must be followed at all cost? Which are expendable? There are poets who follow a strict 5-7-5 syllabic pattern, and include a single seasonal reference. There are poets who do neither, yet still craft equally short, and much shorter poems. In either case, skillful syntax, and important concepts such as "artlessness" (no contrivance), reverence of the commonplace, of the imperfect, of the transitory, and interpenetrating the objects of our inspiration, are essential elements to creating good haiku, and for assimilating the maximum of what haiku has to offer. In other words, the depth and spirit of haiku can be achieved in either approach to form.

Above all, I believe that tradition should be honored, but not to the exclusion of innovation. And I believe that that can be accomplished in several ways. One way is to write haiku in which the 5-7-5 form, and inclusion of a seasonal reference are of paramount importance, yet in which the life and spirit still shine. Another way to evoke haiku spirit is to write poems in which the experience dictates the form, even if the poems don't approximate the 5-7-5 syllabic structure, and/or don't contain a seasonal reference.

There is another way to embody haiku spirit in a poem. It is to amalgamate the practices mentioned above, and I highly recommend this for one simple and important reason: it honors the gift of tradition, at the same time that it honors our own unique, personal experiences. There is more than one way this can be accomplished. One is to begin each poem with a sincere and tenacious attempt to hold to original form (5-7-5 and one seasonal reference). Then ease the grip on "rules" and concentrate completely on the experience. If necessary, the form should be compromised to uphold the truth of the experience, and bring it into the clearest possible focus. Another way is to do the same thing in reverse. Begin by clarifying the experience, even if the result is a short paragraph, or maybe just three words on the page. Then bring what is there into line with traditional form. Sometimes the "bringing into line" will improve the original poem by utilizing the richness of the language, and sometimes it will diminish the poem by adding unnecessary words, or taking away essential words. At this point it must be decided which way the truth of the experience is best represented. To practice writing in this way is to honor the spirit at the heart of haiku, regardless of whether the resultant poem is a haiku or not.

Tenacity in the struggle to stick to traditional form, coupled with determination not to lose the core experience, results in a more thorough digestion and better understanding of the experience, the haiku form, and most importantly, of ourselves. It also results in a more satisfying, readily accessible poem. Whether or not a poem turns out to be 5-7-5, or contain a seasonal reference, is of secondary importance in light of the poet's efforts to learn and to honor tradition, and to balance tradition with his or her unique form of expression.

For these reasons, it is my intention that The Heron's Nest be a journal that presents haiku in which I feel that the poet has made a sincere attempt to honor tradition. To accomplish this, I'll post two streams of work, plus a list of my five favorite poems submitted. The first stream will consist of haiku that have a 5-7-5 structure (give or take a syllable), and contain a single kigo. The second stream will consist of free-style haiku. My primary criteria for the top five haiku will be depth of spirit, and ease of accessibility to that spirit. You may submit at any time, but new postings, and changes to the top five, will be made on a weekly basis. In this way I'll have sufficient time to really sink into each poem.

Thank you for coming to The Heron's Nest. I hope you enjoy what you find and share what you have.

Christopher Herold
